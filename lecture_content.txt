1. What is Agentic AI?
Agentic AI refers to artificial intelligence systems that can act autonomously toward a goal — planning, executing actions, using tools, and adapting based on feedback — without requiring step-by-step human instructions. Unlike a standard chatbot that responds to a single prompt and stops, an AI Agent can reason across multiple steps, call external tools, remember intermediate results, and coordinate with other agents to solve complex tasks.

1.2  Why Agentic AI Matters for Linguistics

2. How AI Agents Work — Core Architecture
Every AI agent follows the Observe -> Think -> Act -> Reflect cycle. Understanding this loop is essential before designing any multi-agent linguistics system.


2.1  Types of Agent Memory

2.2  Agent Communication Patterns
Sequential (Pipeline): Agent 1 -> Agent 2 -> Agent 3 -> Final Output. Each agent hands its result to the next. Simple and predictable.
Hierarchical (Orchestrator + Sub-agents): A master agent delegates tasks to specialist sub-agents and synthesizes their results.
Parallel (Swarm): Multiple agents work simultaneously on different parts of the data, and their outputs are merged.

In our PMDD project, we use a hybrid pattern: sequential pipeline at the macro level (agents 1 to 5) with an orchestrator (Agent 5) that can request re-analysis from any earlier agent if evidence is insufficient.

4. The New Project: Pragmatic Meaning Drift Detector (PMDD)

4.1  What Does PMDD Analyze?
PMDD investigates how the pragmatic meaning, connotative associations, and discourse functions of key lexical items shift across different time periods, genres, or social groups within a text corpus. This is a core question in Historical Pragmatics, Sociolinguistics, and Corpus Linguistics.

Examples of research questions PMDD can answer from a large corpus:
How has the word "community" shifted in its pragmatic use across political speeches from 2000 to 2024?
Do academic research papers in 2010 use hedging language differently from those in 2024?
How do informal social media texts differ from formal news texts in their use of causal connectives?
Is there evidence of register borrowing — formal vocabulary appearing in informal contexts?
How do politeness strategies evolve across different sections of a legal document corpus?
